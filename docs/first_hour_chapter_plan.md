# First Hour Chapter Plan

This is the canonical story and pacing plan for Brave's first hour.

The current mechanical ladder is already strong enough to support a real opening chapter:

`Wayfarer's Yard` -> `Practice Makes Heroes` -> `Rats in the Kettle` -> `Roadside Howls` -> `Fencebreakers` -> `Ruk the Fence-Cutter`

The missing piece is impact. The first hour should not begin as "welcome to the tutorial." It should begin as "the town has just been hit, and you are one of the people close enough to help."

## Recommended Cold Open

Use a cold open called **Lanternfall**.

The first thing a new player should experience is not a calm training prompt. It should be a short, readable crisis:

- A bell hammers across Brambleford before dawn.
- One of the south road lanterns has gone black.
- A cart limps in with cut harness, clawed mud, and fence splinters jammed in the wheels.
- Tamsin grabs the player before panic spreads: get oriented, get kit, prove you can stand, report to Harl.

This creates a blast without changing the existing tutorial spine.

## First-Hour Promise

By the end of the first hour, the player should feel:

- "I learned how Brave works."
- "Brambleford is mine to protect."
- "The road problem was real, not filler."
- "Ruk was the first villain, not just the first boss."
- "The world just opened after a complete first chapter."

## Minute-By-Minute Shape

### 0 To 3 Minutes: Impact

Open with the bell, the dead lantern, and the damaged cart.

Keep it short. The player needs one image, one problem, one person giving direction.

Best first emotional beat: Brambleford is warm, but it is not safe by default.

### 3 To 12 Minutes: Emergency Onboarding

Use the current Wayfarer's Yard tutorial, but frame every command as competent response:

- Tamsin teaches movement because the yard is organizing.
- Nella teaches gear and pack because someone is going outside the walls.
- Brask teaches combat because even small things are stirred up.
- Rest is taught because Brave expects recovery discipline.
- Harl receives the player because the town needs hands, not because the tutorial is done.

The tutorial should stay mechanically gentle. The urgency is narrative, not punitive.

### 12 To 22 Minutes: The Town Problem Is Real

`Practice Makes Heroes` and `Rats in the Kettle` prove that Brambleford's cozy spaces can be threatened.

The cellar should not feel like random MMO rats. Tie it to the cold open:

- rats are agitated by the same wrong lantern pulse
- food stores matter because the road is compromised
- Uncle Pib is funny, but the job still matters

Required feeling: "This town is charming, and trouble is already inside the walls."

### 22 To 40 Minutes: The Road Opens

`Roadside Howls` and `Fencebreakers` move the player from town warmth to first adventure danger.

The road should show escalation in physical evidence:

- pawprints near town
- knife-cut fence posts
- stolen planks
- wagon paint in Ruk's chopping block
- goblins acting like they were sent, not just hungry

Required feeling: "The road is a trail of clues."

### 40 To 60 Minutes: First Boss Payoff

`Ruk the Fence-Cutter` should answer the first-hour question: who hit the road?

Ruk does not need to explain the whole game. He needs to prove that the opening crisis had a face, a weapon, and a camp.

After Ruk falls, give a clear handoff:

- Mira points toward the woods and deeper tracks.
- Joss hints that the south light is stranger than road sabotage.
- The Observatory and Junk-Yard Planet become available as a tempting branch.
- Brambleford acknowledges the win through dialogue or trophy display.

Required feeling: "We finished chapter one, and the map got bigger."

## Story Threads To Plant

Plant only three threads in the first hour:

1. The road was cut deliberately.
2. The lantern went dark in a way the town does not understand.
3. Ruk is connected to something larger, but he is still a complete first villain.

Do not introduce too many proper nouns. The player does not need the full Blackfen or Drowned Weir context yet.

## Delivery Rules

- Lead with vivid action, then teach.
- Keep instructional text inside NPC intent.
- Make every quest handoff name the next person or place.
- Repeat the first-hour mystery through objects: notice board, cellar slate, fence posts, chopping block.
- Use room descriptions as evidence, not lore dumps.
- Let the player act every few lines. Brave is still a MUD, not a visual novel.

## Ruk As Chapter-One Villain

Ruk should read as:

- immediate
- ugly
- practical
- connected to the road damage
- dangerous enough to matter
- small enough to defeat in the first hour

He is not the main villain. He is the first answer.

The best version of Ruk leaves one suspicious object behind: a cold lantern shard, a marked rail, a map scratch, or a road token that points to deeper trouble without stealing the victory.

## Success Criteria

The first hour is working when:

- a new player understands the controls without feeling like they sat through a lesson
- the town has a memorable first image
- the cellar, road, fence line, and Ruk feel connected
- co-op players can join without derailing progression
- Ruk's defeat feels like a real chapter end
- the next branch is obvious without being forced

If the player logs out after Ruk, they should remember an event, not a checklist.
